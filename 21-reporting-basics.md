# Security Reporting Basics

These notes cover the fundamentals of security reporting.
Reporting is a critical part of cybersecurity work,
especially in pentesting, incident response, and Blue Team operations.

The goal of reporting is clarity, accuracy, and usefulness.

---

## 1. Why Reporting Matters

Security work has value only if it is communicated clearly.

Reports are used to:
- explain risk
- document findings
- support decision-making
- guide remediation

Poor reporting can nullify good technical work.

---

## 2. Audience Awareness

Reports are read by different audiences:
- technical teams
- management
- executives
- non-technical stakeholders

A good report balances:
- technical accuracy
- clear language
- business relevance

---

## 3. Types of Security Reports

Common report types:
- penetration testing reports
- incident response reports
- assessment summaries
- post-incident reviews

Each type has different goals,
but structure and clarity always matter.

---

## 4. Report Structure (High-Level)

A typical security report includes:
- executive summary
- scope and context
- findings
- impact analysis
- remediation recommendations
- conclusion

Consistent structure improves readability.

---

## 5. Executive Summary

The executive summary:
- is written for non-technical readers
- highlights key risks
- explains business impact

It should answer:
- what is the risk?
- how serious is it?
- why should we care?

---

## 6. Findings Description

Each finding should include:
- clear title
- description of the issue
- affected systems or components

Descriptions should be:
- factual
- concise
- free of unnecessary jargon

---

## 7. Impact Explanation

Impact explains:
- what could happen
- who is affected
- how severe the consequences are

Impact is more important than
technical complexity.

---

## 8. Evidence and Reproducibility

Reports should include evidence such as:
- logs
- screenshots
- timestamps
- request/response examples

Evidence supports credibility
and helps remediation teams.

---

## 9. Remediation Recommendations

Good recommendations are:
- clear
- actionable
- realistic

They should explain:
- what to fix
- why it matters
- how it reduces risk

Avoid vague advice.

---

## 10. Tone and Professionalism

Reports must be:
- neutral
- objective
- respectful

Avoid:
- blame
- speculation
- emotional language

Professional tone builds trust.

---

## 11. Common Reporting Mistakes

Common issues include:
- unclear language
- missing impact explanation
- excessive technical detail
- lack of prioritization

Avoiding these improves report quality.

---

## 12. Reporting as a Skill

Reporting is a skill that improves with practice.

Strong reporting:
- increases influence
- improves outcomes
- demonstrates professionalism

Technical skill and communication
must go together.

---

## Key Takeaways

- reporting communicates security value
- audience matters
- structure improves clarity
- impact matters more than complexity
- evidence builds trust
- clear remediation drives action
- professionalism is essential
