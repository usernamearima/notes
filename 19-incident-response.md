# Incident Response Basics

These notes cover the fundamentals of Incident Response (IR).
Incident Response focuses on detecting, containing,
and recovering from security incidents in a structured way.

All content is for learning and legal environments only.

---

## 1. What Is an Incident

A security incident is any event that:
- threatens confidentiality
- threatens integrity
- threatens availability

Examples:
- malware infection
- unauthorized access
- data breach
- service disruption

Not every alert is an incident.

---

## 2. Purpose of Incident Response

Incident Response exists to:
- minimize damage
- restore normal operations
- preserve evidence
- prevent recurrence

A structured response reduces chaos.

---

## 3. Incident Response Lifecycle

A common IR lifecycle includes:
1. Preparation
2. Identification
3. Containment
4. Eradication
5. Recovery
6. Lessons Learned

Each phase has a specific goal.

---

## 4. Preparation

Preparation happens before any incident.

Includes:
- defined procedures
- trained staff
- logging and monitoring
- communication plans
- backups

Good preparation reduces response time.

---

## 5. Identification

Identification determines:
- whether an incident is real
- what systems are affected
- how severe the incident is

Sources of identification:
- SIEM alerts
- user reports
- monitoring systems
- threat intelligence

---

## 6. Containment

Containment limits the spread of the incident.

Examples:
- isolating systems
- blocking network traffic
- disabling compromised accounts

Containment may be:
- short-term
- long-term

Goal: stop further damage.

---

## 7. Eradication

Eradication removes the root cause.

Examples:
- removing malware
- closing vulnerabilities
- fixing misconfigurations
- resetting credentials

Eradication ensures the threat is gone.

---

## 8. Recovery

Recovery restores normal operations.

Includes:
- restoring systems from backups
- monitoring for reinfection
- validating system integrity

Systems should return to production
only when safe.

---

## 9. Evidence Handling

Incident Response often requires evidence preservation.

Evidence may include:
- logs
- disk images
- memory captures
- network traffic

Improper handling can destroy evidence.

---

## 10. Communication During Incidents

Clear communication is critical.

Includes:
- internal teams
- management
- legal
- external stakeholders

Poor communication increases impact.

---

## 11. Lessons Learned

After an incident:
- review what happened
- identify gaps
- improve controls
- update procedures

Lessons learned strengthen future defense.

---

## 12. Incident Response Roles

Common roles:
- incident commander
- analysts
- system owners
- communication leads

Clear roles prevent confusion.

---

## Key Takeaways

- incidents threaten CIA principles
- structured response reduces damage
- preparation is critical
- containment limits spread
- eradication removes root cause
- recovery restores operations
- lessons learned improve resilience
